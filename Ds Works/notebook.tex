    \subsection{4. Basic of Grouping}\label{basic-of-grouping}

grouped based on one or several variables and analysis is performed on

    \subsection{5. Correlation and
Causation}\label{correlation-and-causation}

    \textbf{Correlation}: a measure of the extent of interdependence between
variables.

\textbf{Causation}: the relationship between cause and effect between
two variables.

It is important to know the difference between these two and that
correlation does not imply causation. Determining correlation is much
simpler the determining causation as causation may require independent
experimentation

    \subsection{Pearson Correlation}\label{pearson-correlation}

The Pearson Correlation measures the linear dependence between two
variables X and Y. The resulting coefficient is a value between -1 and 1
inclusive, where: - \textbf{1}: total positive linear correlation, -
\textbf{0}: no linear correlation, the two variables most likely do not
affect each other - \textbf{-1}: total negative linear correlation.

    sometimes we would like to know the significant of the correlation
estimate.

    \textbf{P-value}: What is this P-value? The P-value is the probability
value that the correlation between these two variables is statistically
significant. Normally, we choose a significance level of 0.05, which
means that we are 95\% confident that the correlation between the
variables is significant.

By convention, when the - p-value is \textless{} 0.001 we say there is
strong evidence that the correlation is significant, - the p-value is
\textless{} 0.05; there is moderate evidence that the correlation is
significant, - the p-value is \textless{} 0.1; there is weak evidence
that the correlation is significant, and - the p-value is \textgreater{}
0.1; there is no evidence that the correlation is significant.

library.

    \subsection{6. ANOVA}\label{anova}

    \subsubsection{ANOVA: Analysis of
Variance}\label{anova-analysis-of-variance}

The Analysis of Variance (ANOVA) is a statistical method used to test
whether there are significant differences between the means of two or
more groups. ANOVA returns two parameters:

\textbf{F-test score:} ANOVA assumes the means of all groups are the
same, calculates how much the actual means deviate from the assumption,
and reports it as the F-test score. A larger score means there is a
larger difference between the means.

\textbf{P-value:} P-value tells how statistically significant is our
calculated score value

If our price variable is strongly correlated with the variable we are
analyzing, expect ANOVA to return a sizeable F-test score and a small
p-value.

\subsubsection{Drive Wheels}\label{drive-wheels}

variable, the groupby function will come in handy. Because the ANOVA
algorithm averages the data automatically, we do not need to take the
